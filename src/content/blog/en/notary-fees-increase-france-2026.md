When purchasing real estate in France, buyers must account for significant transaction costs, commonly referred to as **notary fees** (*frais de notaire*). In 2026, a major regulatory update takes effect, allowing French departments to increase local property taxes. This change will directly affect the purchasing power and budget requirements of future buyers.

## What is the 2026 DMTO Tax Hike?

Notary fees in France do not actually go entirely to the notary; in fact, about 80% of the total amount consists of taxes collected on behalf of local authorities and the State. These are known as the **Droits de Mutation à Titre Onéreux (DMTO)** or transfer duties.

Previously, the departmental portion of these duties was capped at **4.50%**. However, due to local government budget deficits, the French Finance Act has authorized departments to temporarily increase this cap to **5.00%** starting in 2026, extending through to March 31, 2028.

## How it Affects Your Property Budget

For pre-owned (existing) properties, notary fees historically averaged **7.0% to 8.0%** of the purchase price. With the new 5.00% cap adopted by almost all departments, total notary fees will rise to between **7.5% and 8.5%** of the purchase price.

### Example Cost Calculation:
If you purchase an existing property for **€250,000**:
* **Before the hike (4.5% DMTO):** Transfer duties amounted to €11,250, with total fees around €18,200.
* **After the hike (5.0% DMTO):** Transfer duties rise to €12,500, pushing total fees to approximately €19,450.
* **The difference:** An additional **€1,250** that must be funded directly from your personal capital (*apport personnel*).

## Planning Ahead

Since French banks rarely allow mortgage loans to cover notary fees—usually requiring the buyer to pay these fees out of pocket—this 0.5% tax increase makes it crucial to review your savings target before signing a purchase agreement.
