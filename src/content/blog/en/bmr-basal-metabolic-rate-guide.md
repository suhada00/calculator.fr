Your **Basal Metabolic Rate (BMR)** represents the baseline level of energy (measured in calories) that your body requires to carry out its most essential physiological functions while at absolute rest. This includes cellular processes, breathing, heartbeat regulation, nervous system signaling, and keeping your organs working correctly.

## The Science of BMR

Your body is constantly burning energy, even when you are asleep or inactive. In fact, BMR typically accounts for **60% to 75%** of your total daily calorie burn. 

To estimate this rate, scientists created formulas based on weight, height, age, and sex. The most popular calculation is the revised **Harris-Benedict equation** (Roza and Shizgal, 1984):

* **For Men:** `BMR = 88.362 + (13.397 × weight in kg) + (4.799 × height in cm) - (5.677 × age in years)`
* **For Women:** `BMR = 447.593 + (9.247 × weight in kg) + (3.098 × height in cm) - (4.330 × age in years)`

### Practical Example:
A **30-year-old** male weighing **80 kg** and standing **180 cm** tall has an estimated BMR of:
`BMR = 88.362 + (13.397 × 80) + (4.799 × 180) - (5.677 × 30) = 88.362 + 1071.76 + 863.82 - 170.31 = 1853.63 kcal`

## Key Factors That Influence Your BMR

Several elements affect how fast your body burns energy at rest:
* **Lean Muscle Mass:** Muscle tissue requires far more energy to maintain than fat tissue. Increasing muscle through resistance training is the most effective way to permanently raise your BMR.
* **Biological Sex:** Men generally have a higher BMR than women due to higher levels of testosterone and greater average muscle mass.
* **Age:** As we get older, BMR naturally declines due to age-related muscle loss (*sarcopenia*) and hormone shifts.
