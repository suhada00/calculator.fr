When negotiating a new job contract or discussing a raise in France, employment offers are almost exclusively quoted in **gross salary** (*salaire brut*). However, the actual amount deposited into your bank account is the **net salary** (*salaire net*). The difference represents the social security contributions (*cotisations sociales*) deducted to fund France’s welfare system.

## Gross vs Net: The Basics

* **Gross Salary (*Brut*):** The total compensation agreed upon with the employer before any deductions are made.
* **Net Salary (*Net*):** The take-home pay after deducting social charges (healthcare, retirement pensions, unemployment insurance, CSG, and CRDS). Note that income tax withholding (*prélèvement à la source*) is deducted from this net amount.

## Social Security Contribution Rates in 2026

The percentage of salary deductions varies based on your professional status:
* **Private Sector Employee (Non-executive / *Non-Cadre*):** Social contributions average around **22%** of gross pay. To estimate net, multiply the gross by **0.78**.
* **Private Sector Executive (*Cadre*):** Executive contributions are higher due to additional pension schemes, averaging around **25%** of gross pay. To estimate net, multiply the gross by **0.75**.
* **Civil Servant (*Fonctionnaire*):** Public sector employees pay lower contributions, averaging **15%** of gross. Multiplier: **0.85**.

## Practical Example (Non-cadre):
If your annual gross salary is **€40,000**:
* `Estimated annual net salary = €40,000 × 0.78 = €31,200`
* `Estimated monthly net salary = €31,200 / 12 = €2,600`
