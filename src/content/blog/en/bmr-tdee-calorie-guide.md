Achieving your body composition goals—whether that means losing weight, maintaining weight, or building muscle mass—comes down to a fundamental law of physics: energy balance. To manage this balance, you need to understand two key metrics: **Basal Metabolic Rate (BMR)** and **Total Daily Energy Expenditure (TDEE)**.

## 1. Basal Metabolic Rate (BMR): Rest Day Calories

Your **Basal Metabolic Rate (BMR)** represents the minimum number of calories your body requires to stay alive at complete rest. Even if you spent the entire day lying in bed, your body would still need these calories to power internal functions such as breathing, blood circulation, cellular repair, and hormone regulation.

BMR is calculated based on biological sex, age, height, and weight. The most widely accepted method is the revised **Harris-Benedict Equation** (Roza and Shizgal, 1984):
* **For Men:** `BMR = 88.362 + (13.397 × weight in kg) + (4.799 × height in cm) - (5.677 × age in years)`
* **For Women:** `BMR = 447.593 + (9.247 × weight in kg) + (3.098 × height in cm) - (4.330 × age in years)`

## 2. Total Daily Energy Expenditure (TDEE): Moving Around

Once you get out of bed and move, your energy demands increase. Your **Total Daily Energy Expenditure (TDEE)** includes your BMR multiplied by an activity factor:
* **Sedentary (little to no exercise):** `BMR × 1.2`
* **Lightly Active (light exercise 1-3 days/week):** `BMR × 1.375`
* **Moderately Active (moderate exercise 3-5 days/week):** `BMR × 1.55`
* **Very Active (heavy exercise 6-7 days/week):** `BMR × 1.725`
* **Extrêmement active (intense double training or physical job):** `BMR × 1.9`

## Setting Calorie Goals Based on TDEE

Once you calculate your TDEE, you can adjust your daily food intake to reach your fitness goals:
* **Weight Loss:** Consume a moderate calorie deficit by eating 10% to 20% fewer calories than your TDEE.
* **Muscle Gain:** Consume a light calorie surplus by eating 10% to 15% more calories than your TDEE.
* **Weight Maintenance:** Consume exactly your TDEE calories.
