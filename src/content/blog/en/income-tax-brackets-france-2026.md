Each year, the French Finance Act adjusts the brackets of the progressive income tax (*barème progressif de l'impôt sur le revenu*) to offset inflation and preserve the purchasing power of taxpayers. In 2026, for income earned during the 2025 calendar year, the tax brackets are officially indexed upward by **0.9%**.

## New French Income Tax Brackets for 2026

The income tax bar for 2026 (applied per tax share or *part de quotient familial*) is structured as follows:

1. **Up to €11,624:** Taxed at **0%** (Non-taxable band)
2. **From €11,624 to €29,028:** Taxed at **11%**
3. **From €29,028 to €83,147:** Taxed at **30%**
4. **From €83,147 to €180,346:** Taxed at **41%**
5. **Above €180,346:** Taxed at **45%**

## Why the 0.9% Indexation Matters

Without this adjustment, a worker whose salary rises to match inflation would be pushed into a higher tax bracket, a phenomenon known as bracket creep. As a result, they would pay a higher percentage of their income in tax, despite having no increase in real purchasing power.

Thanks to the **0.9%** indexation, if your income did not increase by more than 0.9% compared to the previous year, your total French income tax liability will slightly decrease or remain stable.

## How to Estimate Your Tax Bill

The French tax calculation involves several variables:
* **Quotient Familial:** Your household shares. A single adult represents 1 share, a married couple represents 2, the first two children add 0.5 shares each, and subsequent children add 1 share each.
* **Standard Deductions:** A default 10% deduction is applied to your salary for professional expenses before the tax brackets are applied (capped at a statutory limit).
* **Progressive Calculation:** Your net taxable income divided by your household shares is run through the progressive bands, and the result is multiplied back by the number of shares.
